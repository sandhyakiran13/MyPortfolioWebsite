Year One of Fellowship

Shivshakti Vidyalaya

Year One School

My first year of fellowship began at Shivshakti Vidyalaya, a low-income private school in Kandivali (W). The school had six classrooms, a small compound, and two toilets, though only one was usable, shared by boys, girls, and staff. I was assigned to teach 28 eighth graders in a classroom that was small and filled to capacity. Despite the limited space and resources, I was excited and ready to take on the challenge of teaching a new group of students, looking forward to learning and growing with them.

Year 1 Students and their 3rd Standard Fellow Shruti

Initial Challenges

Academic Gaps Due to Interrupted Learning

Due to the extended school closures during the 2020 to 2022 lockdown period, many students in 5th and 6th grades experienced significant learning loss, particularly in reading, comprehension, speaking, and writing skills in English. With schools transitioning to online learning, many students were unable to access the same level of support and resources they would have received in a traditional classroom setting. As a result, by the time they reached 8th grade, their reading levels were closer to that of 3rd or 4th graders, leading to a considerable gap in their academic development.

Academic Disparities and Their Impact on Classroom Cohesion

Some students were at lower academic levels due to the challenges mentioned earlier, while others were at relatively higher levels. The students at lower levels often lacked confidence, and the disparity in skill levels created a divide, leading to discrimination and a lack of cohesion within the classroom. This difference in abilities hindered the development of a strong classroom culture or sense of teamwork.

Impact of Inconsistent Teaching

Due to some personal challenges, the teacher for other subjects (Math and Science) had difficulty maintaining a regular presence in school. This occasional discontinuity in instruction created some gaps in the students' education. As I was already teaching English and Social Studies, stepping in to cover other subjects on short notice proved to be challenging for both the students and myself, as it added to their learning loss. While the intent was always to support the students, the absence of consistent teaching in these subjects made it harder to address their academic needs fully.

No Access to Practical Computer Skills

In a world where digital education and computer literacy are increasingly essential, our school faced a significant challenge due to the lack of a functional computer lab. Students were only taught computer skills through textbooks, with little to no hands-on experience or practical application. This limited approach hindered their ability to develop essential digital skills, putting them at a disadvantage in a rapidly evolving technological landscape.

Solution

Differentiated Worksheets

Based on the MOY data, I categorized my students into three groups according to their Reading Comprehension (RC) levels: Dolphins (low rigor), Tigers (medium rigor), and Eagles (high rigor). Initially, I used a uniform approach by creating the same type of RC lesson plans and worksheets for all students. However, I soon realized this method was ineffective in addressing their diverse learning needs. To resolve this, I began developing differentiated worksheets tailored to the specific requirements of each group, ensuring more effective and personalized learning experiences. This approach allowed me to better cater to the individual needs of my students, providing them with the support they needed to improve their reading comprehension skills.

Differentiated Extra Classes

I also began conducting differentiated extra classes to support high-risk students and high-potential learners, helping them bridge the gap in reading comprehension. I used available resources, such as the bridge curriculum, to enhance their learning experience.

Functional School Library

To foster a love for reading, we procured storybooks and bookshelves through donations. With the help of students, we set up a functional school library. After this, we introduced regular DEAR (Drop Everything and Read) blocks to encourage reading habits.

Engaging Social Studies

Throughout the year, I experimented with various methods to make Social Studies (SST) more engaging, as many students disliked the subject. One effective approach was teaching through story maps and graphic organizers. I also introduced mind maps, where students used basic WH questions (Who, What, Where, When, Why, How) to simplify lengthy texts into smaller concepts. The mind maps and graphic organizers helped students understand and articulate answers better so that they could recall them in exams. Link to lesson plan

Promoting Collaboration

To promote collaboration, I created heterogeneous groups with students from different rigor levels. Each student was held accountable for their group's performance. During lessons, I ensured that they engaged in at least one group activity, project, or teamwork task. This helped students interact and bond with each other and grow together regardless of student levels.

Goal Setting

At the beginning of the semester, we conducted a group goal-setting activity where students reflected on their academic aspirations. They wrote down the goals they hoped to achieve and discussed the specific actions they could take to reach those goals. This exercise helped them develop a clearer sense of purpose and accountability for their academic growth.

Debate Sessions

Debate sessions were organized in class, where students were divided into groups of three and given various topics to discuss. One of the topics, Should school uniforms be compulsory? sparked an engaging discussion. The activity encouraged students to express their opinions openly and confidently.

The Struggle for Comfort: Why Girls' School Uniforms Should Be Reconsidered.

-Jyoti Chauhan

Jyoti Chauhan opposed making school uniforms compulsory. She pointed out that students face corporal punishment for not wearing uniforms, causing stress. The high cost means families can only afford two pairs, leading to hygiene issues. Her strongest argument was the discomfort girls face with frock-style uniforms, especially during menstruation, which makes simple tasks like sitting or climbing stairs difficult and uncomfortable. Exposure

Digital Literacy with Bloomberg

To address the digital divide and lack of computer literacy among students, a collaboration with Bloomberg was established. Volunteers conducted sessions to teach students how to use computers and laptops effectively. Over 4-5 sessions at Bloomberg’s office, students learned skills such as using Microsoft Excel and Google Docs, which boosted their confidence in handling technology and improved their performance in computer exams.

Visit to Nehru Science Centre

Students were also taken to the Nehru Science Centre, where they explored scientific projects, tools, and physics experiments. This visit ignited their curiosity and made Science and Math more engaging by connecting concepts to real-life applications.

Live Musical Performance

For the first time, the students attended a live musical performance at the Nita Ambani Cultural Centre, where they watched The Sound of Music. It was an unforgettable experience, and the students thoroughly enjoyed it. Following the event, they were encouraged to write about their experiences, helping them reflect on and express their excitement and learning from the performance.

SEL Support

For students requiring psychological support, a partnership with Atma Prakash facilitated a mental health session, and a counselor was arranged to provide individual counseling sessions weekly over six months. This intervention led to noticeable behavioral improvements. Additionally, a project was designed to address challenges like low self-esteem, exclusion, peer pressure, and lack of confidence. Weekly activities like entangling human knot, obstacle challenge, making the tallest tower with newspapers, etc were conducted, followed by reflection and learning discussions, creating a positive impact on the students’ emotional and mental well-being.

Counselor Support

At the beginning of the year, one of my students, Rizwan, was feeling quite uneasy, which made it difficult for him to come to school. However, after receiving consistent support through counseling, he showed significant progress. Over time, he became more confident, started attending school regularly, and seemed much more secure. Another student, Deepak, gradually became more engaged in class and began initiating conversations with me. It’s been truly rewarding to see both of them grow and become more active and connected in the school environment.

Lows

During my first year as a teacher, I faced the challenge of balancing overwhelming responsibilities with limited support. Often, I found myself managing the class, addressing behavioral issues, and handling additional tasks alone, which was particularly difficult as a new teacher still learning the ropes. This lack of guidance made the experience both exhausting and isolating. I also encountered situations where I had to stand up for ethical practices. Taking action against certain concerning behaviors and advocating for the well-being of students was necessary but led to strained relationships with colleagues and a lack of support from the staff. This made it even harder to navigate the already challenging environment.

Despite these challenges, I still enjoyed being with my students, finding joy and purpose in our interactions. However, the cumulative pressure and emotional toll eventually led me to change schools and start my fellowship in a new environment—one where I could grow and thrive with better alignment to my values.
